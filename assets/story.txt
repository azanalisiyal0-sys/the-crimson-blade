The air inside the wayside tavern tasted of stale sake and the acrid bite of burning charcoal. Thick smoke clung to the low-hanging cedar beams, blurring the edges of the room into a grey haze. In the furthest corner, tucked away from the few remaining patrons, Kenji sat motionless.

He had his kimono slumped off one shoulder, exposing a jagged tear in the muscle of his upper arm. The wound wasn't deep, but it was angry, rimmed with a dull purple bruise. 

Tomoe worked in silence. Her fingers were steady, smelling of crushed herbs and sharp alcohol. She pressed a clean cloth into the cut, and Kenji’s jaw tightened, a small muscle leaping in his cheek, but he didn't make a sound. He didn't look at her; instead, his gaze was fixed on the katana resting across his lap. The *tsuba* was rusted, a pitted circle of iron that looked more like a discarded tool than a warrior's weapon. But the steel beneath the wrap was the Crimson Blade, and it felt heavy—not with weight, but with the memory of the blood it had tasted.

Tomoe pulled the bandage tight. For a heartbeat, her hand lingered on his skin, her thumb brushing against the edge of a jagged scar that ran from his shoulder toward his chest. It wasn't a gesture of romance, but of recognition. They were two broken things clinging to each other in a dying winter.

"You're shaking," she whispered. Her voice was low, barely audible over the crackle of the *irori* hearth.



"I'm cold," Kenji replied.

"You're lying."

He finally looked at her. Her eyes were dark and perceptive, the eyes of someone who spent her days looking for the hidden source of a fever. She knew he wasn't cold. He was coiled.

"We can't stay here," she said, pulling back to gather her medical kit. "The snow is blocking the mountain passes. If they’ve found the trail—"

"They have," Kenji interrupted.

The tavern had gone quiet. It wasn't a sudden silence, but a gradual draining of sound. The low murmur of the other guests had vanished, replaced by the oppressive whistle of the wind leaking through the gaps in the walls. Then came the sound: the rhythmic, heavy crunch of boots on frozen mud.

Kenji didn't move, but his hand drifted toward the hilt of his sword.

Outside, a horse neighed, a sharp, panicked sound that cut through the frost. The heavy wooden door of the tavern creaked open, admitting a swirl of snowflakes and a blast of killing cold. 

A man stepped inside. He wasn't dressed in the flamboyant silks of a high-ranking samurai, but in the functional, dark leather of a hunter—or a tracker. Behind him stood three others, their faces obscured by straw capes, their eyes scanning the dim room with a clinical detachment.

The lead man stopped. He didn't shout. He didn't draw his weapon. He simply stood there, letting the cold air roll off his shoulders.

"The village elder said a man with a rusted guard passed through two days ago," the tracker said. His voice was dry, like parchment rubbing together. "He also mentioned a woman who knows how to stitch a wound."

Kenji felt the air in the room thicken. He didn't look at the men; he looked at the exit behind them. Too narrow. Too many of them.

"We are just travelers," Tomoe said, her voice steady, though she had stepped slightly closer to Kenji.

The tracker smiled, a thin, mirthless expression. "Lord Kurada doesn't care for travelers. He cares for lost property. And that blade," he nodded toward the katana on Kenji's lap, "is very expensive property."

Kenji’s grip tightened on the hilt. He could feel the ghost of a memory: the smell of burning cedar and the sound of Kazuma’s laughter as he had turned his back on their oath. Kazuma wouldn't be here—he was too comfortable in Kurada’s courts now—but he had sent his dogs.

"Leave," Kenji said.

"I can't do that. The magistrate's orders are quite specific. He wants the sword returned. He didn't say anything about the hand holding it remaining attached."

The tracker signaled. The three men behind him moved with a synchronized, predatory grace. They didn't charge; they drifted, closing the circle, their hands resting on the pommels of their shorter blades.

The violence happened in a blur of grey and red.

One of the scouts lunged, a quick, stabbing motion aimed at Kenji’s midsection. Kenji didn't stand; he pivoted on his heel, the movement fluid and economical. The Crimson Blade left its scabbard with a singular, metallic *shring*. 

The steel caught the dim light of the hearth for a fraction of a second before it carved a diagonal line across the scout's throat. There was no cinematic spray—just a wet, bubbling sound and the sudden, heavy thud of a body hitting the tatami mats.

The tracker hadn't moved, but his eyes had narrowed. He drew his blade slowly. "You've grown slow, Kenji. The weight of your failure is making you clumsy."

Kenji didn't answer. He stood now, his breath fogging in the air, his gaze vacant. He wasn't fighting for honor or for the magistrate's gold. He was fighting because if he stopped, the silence of the dead would finally catch up to him.

The tracker attacked with a sudden, explosive burst of speed. The blades clashed—*clang*—sending sparks flying into the smoke. The sound echoed through the small room, vibrating in the teeth of the remaining patrons. They traded three rapid strikes, the steel screaming as it slid against steel.

Kenji felt the sting of a shallow cut across his cheek. He didn't flinch. He waited for the tracker to overextend, for that one millisecond of imbalance.

It came. The tracker pushed too hard on a downward strike. Kenji pivoted, sliding his blade upward in a brutal, short arc. The edge entered under the tracker's ribs, sliding through the diaphragm and piercing the heart.

The tracker froze. He looked down at the blade protruding from his chest, then up at Kenji. His expression wasn't one of fear, but of mild annoyance.

"He... knows... where you are," the tracker wheezed, a bubble of blood popping on his lips.

Kenji ripped the blade out with a wet *schlick*. He didn't ask who. He knew.

"Kenji!" Tomoe’s voice snapped him back.

He turned. Through the open door, the twilight had turned a bruised purple. In the distance, at the edge of the village, a line of torches was emerging from the tree line. Dozens of them. A rhythmic beat of drums began to pulse through the frozen air—the steady, bureaucratic march of Lord Kurada’s personal guard.

They hadn't just sent scouts. They had sent a harvesting party.

"We have to go. Now," Tomoe said, grabbing his arm. Her grip was tight, her knuckles white.

Kenji looked at the bodies on the floor, then at the approaching fire. He sheathed the Crimson Blade, the rusted *tsuba* clicking back into place. The fight had been brief, but the exhaustion hit him all at once, a crushing weight that settled in his marrow.

He grabbed Tomoe’s hand and pulled her toward the back exit, stepping out into the biting wind. As they vanished into the white veil of the falling snow, the first torchlight hit the tavern walls, casting long, dancing shadows that looked like reaching fingers. 

They were no longer just running from a magistrate; they were running from a ghost who knew exactly how they bled.
